From the Marshes

A Day in the Life: Hunting in the River

March 15, 99 million years ago by Spinosaurus

The river is calm today, but I know what lurks beneath the surface—giant fish, my primary source of food. With my long, crocodile-like snout, I wait patiently. This morning, I caught a 6-foot Onchopristis—a tricky catch, but its barbed snout is no match for my powerful jaws. Being semi-aquatic gives me an edge that most other dinosaurs lack. Fish aren’t the only thing out here though. I spotted a Sarcosuchus prowling the shallows. A fight between us would be brutal, but for now, we keep our distance.

In these times, food is plentiful, but the seasons are changing, and soon, my territory will be tested. Smaller predators like the Carcharodontosaurus are a constant threat, though they rarely challenge me directly. I will need to travel farther upstream soon, where the fish are more abundant, and fewer rivals roam.

Reflections on My Rivals

“Carcharodontosaurus may be fast, but in the water, I reign supreme.”

My largest competitor on land, Carcharodontosaurus, is swift and deadly, with sharp teeth that can slice through prey in seconds. However, they are not adept in water, and that's where I have the upper hand. Territorial disputes are inevitable, but as long as I control the rivers, my supremacy remains uncontested. It’s a delicate balance of power.

Lessons from the Hunt

Over time, I’ve learned to adapt. Fishing is an art, and patience is key. Here's what I’ve gathered:

  • Stay close to the riverbanks where fish congregate.
  • Use my sail to regulate my body temperature while waiting for prey.
  • Strike swiftly, as any commotion in the water can scare the fish away.

Each hunt brings new challenges, but my instincts never fail me.

Seasons in the Wetlands

The wetlands are ever-changing. As the dry season approaches, the river shrinks, concentrating fish and forcing predators closer together. It is a time of tension. Migrating to safer grounds becomes essential, but leaving my territory behind is always a risk. I must be cautious of new rivals staking their claim while I am gone.

The Dry Season: A Time of Scarcity

May 10, 99 million years ago by Spinosaurus

The dry season is brutal. The once-vast rivers have become shallow streams, and the fish have dwindled. The wetlands are now teeming with desperate predators. Some leave in search of food, but I stay. My size and strength give me the advantage over smaller dinosaurs, and my ability to catch what few fish remain means I can survive here—barely. It’s a dangerous gamble, but one I must take to defend my territory.

"A Spinosaurus without its waters is like a fish out of water—lost and vulnerable."

Survival Tactics

  • Stay close to the deepest parts of the river, where fish are more likely to remain.
  • Conserve energy by hunting only when necessary.
  • Avoid unnecessary confrontations with other large predators—it's a waste of energy.
